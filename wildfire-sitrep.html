Predictive Services Discussion

Red Flag Warnings for wind gusts up to 45 mph with low relative humidity in the teens are in effect for Monday across portions of northeast California, southeastern Oregon, and northwest Nevada as a low-pressure trough moves in from the northwest. Further west, west of the Cascades in Washington and Oregon, measurable rainfall with some wetting amounts of a quarter of an inch to an inch are expected.

East of the Cascades and across much of the Great Basin and Desert Southwest minimum relative humidities will be mostly in the teens. Across the Rockies isolated to widely scattered mixed wet & dry thunderstorms are likely with the greatest coverage likely to be across northern Idaho and western Montana as well as across portions of central & eastern Arizona into New Mexico. Wetting rains of a quarter of an inch to an inch are likely across much of New Mexico.

A swath of showers & thunderstorms is also expected along the Front Range of the Rockies & southern High Plains from New Mexico & Texas north to northeast across Nebraska & adjacent areas. Meanwhile, a slow-moving frontal system extending from the Gulf Coast northeastward into the Northeast is likely to bring widespread quarter of an inch amounts up to as much as 2 inches for most areas from the Southeast northward all the way across northern Maine.

Source: National Predictive Services, NIFC — May 25, 2026

SPC Day 1 Fire Weather Outlook
Issued 1257 AM CDT Tue May 26, 2026  ·  Valid: May 26 1200Z – May 27 1200Z
Elevated to Locally Critical
Great Basin
A robust upper-level low shifting southward into the Great Basin combined with a deepening surface low will produce a strengthening pressure gradient. Ahead of an eastward-progressing cold front, sustained southwesterly surface winds of 20–30 mph are expected. A deeply mixed and dry boundary layer will support RH reductions to 15–20% in combination with cured and drying fine fuels, primarily at lower elevation and mid-slope areas. Elevated to locally critical fire weather threat.
Elevated
Northeastern Montana & Extreme Western North Dakota
As the upper-level trough progresses southeastward, pronounced lee surface trough development is expected across central MT. Southeasterly winds will accelerate to 15–20 mph across much of the northern High Plains. Limited low-level moisture return will support RH reductions to 15–20% during peak afternoon heating. Delayed green-up and existing dry fuels align with these dry and breezy conditions. Afternoon instability may support isolated thunderstorm development with some dry thunderstorm threat, though sufficient green-up may otherwise mitigate a broader risk.
